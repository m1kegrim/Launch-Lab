Overview & Biology

Octopuses have no skeleton. Zero rigid bones. Yet they can open jars, manipulate tools, and exert extraordinary grip force — all using a structure called a muscular hydrostat. Their arms contain longitudinal muscles (for shortening), transverse muscles (for elongation), and oblique muscles (for twisting). By contracting different muscle groups, they produce complex 3D motion from a boneless limb.

Your robotic arm mimics this using a different mechanism: cable-driven articulation. Instead of muscles, you use tendons (fishing line or Kevlar thread) routed through a chain of 3D-printed vertebrae-like segments. When a servo motor pulls a tendon, the arm curls. Multiple tendons at different positions around the arm allow curling in different directions — just like an octopus choosing which muscle group to contract.

Why Soft Robotics Matters

Traditional rigid robots are great in factories but terrible in unstructured environments — homes, disaster sites, the human body. Soft robots conform to irregular shapes, handle delicate objects without crushing them, and survive impacts that would shatter rigid arms. NASA is researching soft grippers for satellite servicing. Surgical companies are developing tentacle-like endoscopes. This is a rapidly growing field — and you're building its fundamental mechanism.

Design Principles

The Vertebrae Chain

The arm consists of 10–15 identical disc-shaped segments (vertebrae) stacked and connected by a central flexible spine. Each vertebra has 3 or 4 holes near its perimeter where tendons pass through. The spacing between vertebrae determines the arm's flexibility — closer spacing means smoother, more compliant bending; wider spacing means larger range of motion per segment.

How Cable-Driving Works

Imagine the arm standing vertically. Three tendons run from the base, through every vertebra's perimeter holes, to the tip. When you pull Tendon A (at the "front"), the front side of every gap closes — the arm bends forward. Pull Tendon B (at 120° from A), it bends that direction. Pull two tendons simultaneously, and the arm bends in a compound direction. Release all tendons, and the elastic spine snaps everything back to neutral.

CAD Modeling

We'll design the parts in Fusion 360 (free for students). If you've never used Fusion, complete P-004: Intro to CAD first.

Design the Vertebra

Create a new sketch on the XY plane. Draw a circle: diameter 35mm. Add a center circle: diameter 6mm (spine bore). Add 3 circles at 120° intervals on a 24mm diameter bolt circle: each 2mm diameter (tendon channels). Extrude the profile 8mm. Add 0.5mm chamfers on all edges — this helps 3D printing quality and prevents the vertebrae from catching on each other.

Design the Base Plate

The base plate is a larger disc (60mm diameter, 10mm thick) with mounting holes for the servo brackets. It has the same center bore and tendon holes as the vertebrae, plus four M3 mounting holes in a square pattern for bolting to a surface. Include channels/grooves on the bottom face to route the tendons to the servos cleanly.

Design the Tip Cap

The tip cap is a dome-shaped end piece where all tendons terminate. It has a center hole for anchoring the elastic spine and three small anchor holes where you'll knot and glue each tendon. Make it 35mm diameter, 12mm tall with a hemispheric top surface — this lets the arm grip rounded objects better.

Design the Servo Mount Bracket

Design an L-bracket that holds an MG996R servo with its horn pointing upward. The horn is where you'll attach each tendon. Include holes matching the servo's mounting pattern (use calipers to measure your servos). You need 3 of these, arranged around the base plate at 120° intervals.

Post-Print Cleanup

After printing, run a 2mm drill bit through every tendon hole and a 6mm bit through every center bore. Layer lines and slight warping can tighten these holes. The tendons and spine cord must pass through freely with zero friction — any binding kills your arm's performance.

Assembly

Thread the Elastic Spine

Cut a piece of 4mm elastic bungee cord about 15cm longer than your total arm length. Thread it through the center bore of all 12 vertebrae, then through the base plate and tip cap. Knot both ends firmly and add a dab of super glue to each knot. The spine should hold the vertebrae together under light tension — when you compress them and release, they should spring back to even spacing.

Thread the Tendons

Cut three lengths of braided fishing line, each about 50cm (you'll trim later). Thread each one through its corresponding tendon hole in the base plate, up through all 12 vertebrae, and tie it off at the tip cap's anchor hole. Use a surgeon's knot (double overhand) and seal with super glue. The tendon should slide freely through every vertebra when you pull from the base.

Label your tendons: A, B, C (mark with different colored tape at the base end).

Mount the Servos

Screw each servo into its bracket using the servo's included mounting hardware. Bolt the three brackets to the base plate, evenly spaced at 120°. Each servo horn should face inward toward the base plate's tendon exit holes.

Attach Tendons to Servo Horns

Route each tendon from the base plate to its corresponding servo horn. Thread the line through a hole near the horn's tip and tie it off with enough tension that the arm is just barely neutral (straight) when the servo is at its center position (90°). The horn radius determines your mechanical advantage — a hole farther from center gives more arm travel per servo degree but less pulling force.

Servo Control Code

Upload this to your Arduino. It lets you control each servo independently via serial commands, or run preset motion patterns.

// Launch Lab — Tentacle Arm Controller v1.0 #include <Servo.h> Servo servoA, servoB, servoC; const int PIN_A = 9, PIN_B = 10, PIN_C = 11; const int NEUTRAL = 90; void setup() { Serial.begin(9600); servoA.attach(PIN_A); servoB.attach(PIN_B); servoC.attach(PIN_C); center(); Serial.println("Tentacle Arm Ready."); Serial.println("Commands: A[angle] B[angle] C[angle]"); Serial.println(" e.g. 'A45' moves servo A to 45 deg"); Serial.println(" 'W' = wave pattern"); Serial.println(" 'G' = grip (all servos pull)"); Serial.println(" 'R' = release (center all)"); } void loop() { if (Serial.available() > 0) { char cmd = Serial.read(); if (cmd == 'A' || cmd == 'B' || cmd == 'C') { int angle = Serial.parseInt(); angle = constrain(angle, 0, 180); if (cmd == 'A') servoA.write(angle); if (cmd == 'B') servoB.write(angle); if (cmd == 'C') servoC.write(angle); Serial.print(cmd); Serial.print(" → "); Serial.println(angle); } else if (cmd == 'W') wave(); else if (cmd == 'G') grip(); else if (cmd == 'R') center(); } } void center() { servoA.write(NEUTRAL); servoB.write(NEUTRAL); servoC.write(NEUTRAL); Serial.println("Centered."); } void grip() { for (int a = NEUTRAL; a >= 30; a -= 2) { servoA.write(a); servoB.write(a); servoC.write(a); delay(20); } Serial.println("Gripping."); } void wave() { Serial.println("Waving..."); for (int cycle = 0; cycle < 3; cycle++) { for (int i = 0; i <= 60; i += 2) { float rad = i * 0.1; servoA.write(NEUTRAL + 40 * sin(rad)); servoB.write(NEUTRAL + 40 * sin(rad + 2.09)); servoC.write(NEUTRAL + 40 * sin(rad + 4.19)); delay(30); } } center(); }
Power Warning

Do NOT power servos from the Arduino's 5V pin — three MG996R servos can draw over 3A under load, which will brown out or damage the Arduino. Use a separate 5V 3A power supply. Connect the supply's GND to the Arduino's GND (common ground). Only the signal wires go to Arduino pins.

Testing & Measurement

Range of Motion Test

Send individual servo commands (A30, A150, etc.) and observe the arm's deflection. Measure the tip displacement with a ruler. Record the angle-to-displacement relationship for each tendon. If the arm curls unevenly, check that all tendons have equal initial tension.

Lift Capacity Test

This is the big one. Weigh the arm assembly on a kitchen scale (it should be roughly 80–120g). Then test its grip capacity:

  1. Curl the arm around a vertical rod using the grip command.
  2. Hang progressively heavier weights from a hook attached to the arm's tip.
  3. Record the maximum weight before the arm slips or the servos stall.
  4. Calculate the strength-to-weight ratio: lifted weight / arm weight.

Our version lifted 260x its own weight. The key factors: low-stretch braided line (PE braid stretches less than 1%), high-torque servos, and tight vertebra tolerances so the arm wraps snugly around objects.

Troubleshooting

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Arm doesn't return to straightElastic spine too looseReplace with tighter bungee cord or shorten it (re-knot). The spine should hold light tension in neutral.
Uneven curlingUnequal tendon tensionsWith all servos at 90°, adjust each tendon so the arm is perfectly straight. Mark and re-tie as needed.
Servo buzzing / vibratingServo fighting against excessive tensionReduce initial tendon tension. Check that the servo isn't being commanded past its physical range.
Tendon snapsToo much load; knot failureUse 80lb+ braided line. Always secure knots with super glue. Check for sharp edges on vertebra holes that could cut the line.

Poor grip on objectsNot enough vertebrae, or gaps too largeAdd more vertebrae (try 15-18 for longer arm). Reduce gap spacing for smoother conformity around objects.

Going Further

Add a Gripper Tip

Design a 3-finger soft gripper that attaches to the tip cap. Use flexible TPU filament for the fingers. Now you have a full tentacle manipulator that can curl AND grasp.

Pressure Sensing

Embed thin force-sensitive resistors (FSRs, ~$3 each) in the tip cap to measure grip force. Feed the data back to Arduino and implement closed-loop grip control — the arm squeezes until it hits a target force, then holds. This is how surgical robots prevent tissue damage.

Joystick Control

Add a 2-axis joystick module (~$2). Map the X/Y axes to servo A/B commands, and a button to toggle grip. Now you have real-time manual control that feels like piloting a robot.

Document & Compete

A bioinspired robot arm with quantified performance data (strength-to-weight ratio, range of motion, grip force) is a strong science fair project. Film the arm lifting objects, curling around pipes, picking up irregularly-shaped items. The visual impact alone will set your project apart.
