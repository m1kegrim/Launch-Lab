Overview

Put a thin glass tube in water. The water climbs up the tube — on its own, against gravity. That's capillary action. It seems like a simple science-class demo, but it's one of the most important phenomena in space engineering. On the International Space Station, where gravity is effectively zero, capillary forces are the dominant force controlling how fluids move. Understanding capillary action is critical to designing fuel tanks, life support water systems, cooling loops, and plant-growing systems in space.

This is Real NASA Research

NASA's Capillary Flow Experiment (CFE) ran on the ISS from 2010–2015, studying exactly this phenomenon. The challenge: in zero-g, fluids don't settle to the bottom of a tank. They cling to walls, form floating blobs, and move in unexpected ways. Engineers need accurate models of this behavior to design reliable spacecraft fluid systems. Your experiment investigates the same fundamental physics.

The Physics of Capillary Action

Surface Tension

Water molecules attract each other (cohesion). At the surface, molecules have no neighbors above them — so they're pulled inward and sideways more strongly than outward. This creates a "skin" on the water surface that resists being stretched. That's surface tension. It's measured in milliNewtons per meter (mN/m). Water at room temperature: ~72 mN/m.

Adhesion vs. Cohesion

Cohesion is water attracting water. Adhesion is water attracting the tube wall (glass, in our case). When adhesion is stronger than cohesion — which it is for water on glass — the water "wets" the glass and climbs up the wall, pulling the bulk liquid along with it. When cohesion is stronger (like mercury on glass), the liquid depresses instead of climbing.

The Jurin Height Equation

The height that a liquid climbs in a capillary tube is given by:

h = (2γ cos θ) / (ρgr) Where: h = capillary rise height (meters) γ = surface tension (N/m) — water ≈ 0.072 N/m θ = contact angle (degrees) — water on glass ≈ 20° ρ = liquid density (kg/m³) — water = 1000 g = gravitational acceleration (m/s²) — 9.81 r = tube inner radius (meters) Example: glass tube with 0.5mm inner radius h = (2 × 0.072 × cos(20°)) / (1000 × 9.81 × 0.0005) h = 0.1354 / 4.905 h = 0.0276 m ≈ 27.6 mm Key insight: h is inversely proportional to r. Thinner tube → higher rise. In zero gravity (g → 0), h → infinity. That's why fluids behave so differently in space.

Why NASA Cares

In microgravity, the g term in the Jurin equation effectively drops to zero. This means capillary forces — which are negligible at Earth gravity for large containers — become the dominant force governing fluid position and flow. This creates problems:

  • Fuel management: In a spacecraft fuel tank, propellant doesn't settle to the bottom. It can float anywhere, potentially uncovering the fuel intake. NASA uses "propellant management devices" (PMDs) — vanes and screens that use capillary action to guide fuel to the intake.
  • Life support: Water recycling systems on the ISS must move water through filters and membranes without gravity. Capillary action drives much of this flow.
  • Thermal control: Heat pipes in spacecraft use capillary wicking to circulate coolant — no pump needed.
  • Plant growth: Watering plants in space is surprisingly hard. Water doesn't drain "down" to roots. NASA's Veggie experiment uses capillary mats to wick water to plant roots.

Experiment 1: Capillary Rise

The fundamental experiment. Measure how high water climbs in tubes of different diameters and compare to the theoretical prediction.

Prepare the Tubes

Clean every glass tube with rubbing alcohol and let them dry completely. Any residual oils or dust will alter the contact angle and invalidate your results. Handle tubes by the ends — fingerprints deposit oils on the inner surface.

Set Up the Measurement Station

Fill the glass dish with water colored with 2–3 drops of food coloring (makes the meniscus easier to see). Tape a mm ruler vertically next to where you'll place the tubes. Use binder clips to hold each tube upright, or build a simple cardboard stand with holes.

Measure Capillary Rise

Lower each tube vertically into the water, just touching the surface. Wait 30 seconds for the water to reach equilibrium. Measure the height of the water inside the tube above the water level in the dish. Read from the bottom of the meniscus (the curved surface). Record to the nearest 0.5mm.

Repeat with each tube diameter: 0.5mm, 1mm, 1.5mm, 2mm.

Record and Compare

Create a data table and compare your measurements to the theoretical predictions from the Jurin equation:

EXPECTED vs. MEASURED (water on glass, θ ≈ 20°): Tube ID (mm) Predicted h (mm) Your Measured h (mm) ────────────────────────────────────────────────────── 0.5 55.2 _______ 1.0 27.6 _______ 1.5 18.4 _______ 2.0 13.8 _______ Plot: h (y-axis) vs. 1/r (x-axis) Expected: a straight line through the origin. If your line is straight → you've experimentally confirmed the Jurin equation.

Experiment 2: Contact Angle

The contact angle (θ) determines how strongly a liquid wets a surface. You can change it by changing the liquid or the surface.

Test Different Liquids

Using the same 1mm tube, measure capillary rise for:

  • Pure water: θ ≈ 20° on glass
  • Soapy water: Soap is a surfactant — it reduces surface tension from ~72 to ~25 mN/m. Predict: much lower rise.
  • Rubbing alcohol (isopropyl): Surface tension ≈ 23 mN/m, but density is lower (~786 kg/m³) and contact angle is near 0°. Compare to prediction.

Observe the Meniscus Shape

Take close-up photos (use your phone's macro mode or zoom) of the meniscus in each liquid. Water on glass forms a concave meniscus (curves up at the edges). Soapy water will have a flatter meniscus. If you can find a plastic tube, water on plastic has a different contact angle — the meniscus shape changes visibly.

Why Contact Angle Matters in Space

Spacecraft designers carefully choose tank materials and surface coatings to control contact angle. A hydrophilic coating (low θ) makes fuel cling to walls and flow toward intakes via capillary action. A hydrophobic coating (high θ) prevents unwanted wetting. Getting this wrong can mean fuel starvation during a burn — which means a mission-ending failure.

Experiment 3: Simulated Microgravity

This is the one that connects your kitchen table to NASA's parabolic flight. You're going to create brief periods of microgravity and observe how capillary behavior changes.

Build a Drop Capsule

Take a small clear plastic bottle (a mini water bottle works). Fill it about 1/3 with colored water. Insert a capillary tube vertically into the water through a hole in the cap (seal with hot glue or clay). The tube should stick up above the water level inside the sealed bottle.

At rest (1g), the water rises to a certain height in the capillary tube — measure and record this.

Drop It

Hold the bottle at chest height. Set your phone to slow-motion video (240fps if available) and aim it at the capillary tube. Drop the bottle. During the ~0.4 seconds of freefall (before it hits the ground), the water inside experiences near-zero gravity.

What you should observe: the water in the capillary tube rises significantly higher — potentially climbing all the way up the tube and overflowing at the top. This is because the 'g' term in the Jurin equation drops toward zero during freefall, so the equilibrium height shoots upward.

Improve the Setup

Drop from higher: stand on a step stool or use a second-floor window (with a soft landing pad below — cushions, foam). More height = longer freefall = more time for capillary rise to reach a new equilibrium. From 2 meters, you get about 0.64 seconds of microgravity.

Alternatively, build a simple drop rig: suspend the bottle from a string, cut the string (or release a clip). Film with slow-motion. Review frame by frame to measure how the meniscus moves during the drop.

Filming Tip

Use your phone's slow-motion mode. At 240fps, your 0.4-second drop becomes a 3.2-second slow-motion clip — enough to see the meniscus moving. Put a white piece of paper behind the bottle for contrast. Use food coloring to make the water clearly visible. Frame the shot tight on just the capillary tube.

Graphing

Create these graphs (by hand or in a spreadsheet):

  1. h vs. r (tube radius): Should show a 1/r curve (hyperbola).
  2. h vs. 1/r: Should be a straight line through the origin. The slope equals 2γcos(θ)/(ρg). From the slope, you can back-calculate the surface tension of your water — and check it against the known value.
  3. For Experiment 2: Bar chart comparing capillary rise for water, soapy water, and alcohol in the same tube.
  4. For Experiment 3: Side-by-side slow-motion frames showing meniscus position at 0ms, 100ms, 200ms, 300ms of freefall.

Calculating Surface Tension from Your Data

From your h vs. 1/r graph: slope = 2γcos(θ) / (ρg) Rearrange: γ = slope × ρ × g / (2 × cos(θ)) If your slope is 0.0000138 m² (13.8 mm·mm) and θ ≈ 20°: γ = 0.0000138 × 1000 × 9.81 / (2 × cos(20°)) γ = 0.1354 / 1.879 γ = 0.072 N/m = 72 mN/m (Published value for water at 25°C: 72.0 mN/m) If your answer is within 10% — your experiment is solid.

The Zero-G Connection

In May 2026, Michael's experiment flies on a NASA parabolic flight — a modified aircraft that flies repeated parabolas, creating 20–25 second windows of near-zero gravity. During each parabola, the experiment measures capillary rise in tubes of different diameters and surface coatings. The question: does the Jurin equation accurately predict capillary behavior when g drops to 0.01g?

Your ground experiment establishes the 1g baseline. The zero-g flight tests the same physics in a fundamentally different regime. If the Jurin equation holds in both environments, it validates the models that spacecraft designers use. If it doesn't — that's even more interesting, because it means there's physics we don't fully understand yet.

Going Further

Temperature Dependence

Surface tension decreases as temperature increases. Repeat Experiment 1 with cold water (from the fridge, ~5°C), room temperature (~22°C), and warm water (~50°C). Plot capillary rise vs. temperature. You should see the rise decrease as temperature increases — because γ decreases.

Non-Circular Channels

Capillary action doesn't just work in round tubes. Cut narrow channels in acrylic or wood and test whether capillary flow follows different rules. Square channels, V-grooves, and wedge shapes all create different flow patterns — and these are exactly the geometries used in spacecraft PMDs (propellant management devices).

Build a Capillary Pump

Stack multiple capillary tubes side-by-side to create a "capillary pump" — a device that moves water from one reservoir to another using only surface tension. No electricity needed. This is the principle behind certain microgravity water management systems and paper-based microfluidics (used in medical diagnostics like pregnancy tests and COVID rapid tests).
