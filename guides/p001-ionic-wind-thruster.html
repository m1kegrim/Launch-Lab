Overview & Theory

An ionic wind thruster (also called an electrohydrodynamic thruster or lifter) uses high voltage to ionize air molecules, accelerating them to produce thrust — with zero moving parts. The effect was first documented by Thomas Townsend Brown in the 1920s, and today, NASA's research into electroaerodynamic propulsion is reviving interest in this technology for silent, fuel-less flight.

Your thruster will use a thin corona wire (the emitter) held at high voltage, and a larger collector electrode below it. When the voltage is high enough (~20–30 kV), the electric field at the sharp wire strips electrons from nearby air molecules, creating positive ions. These ions accelerate toward the collector, colliding with neutral air molecules along the way, transferring momentum. That cascade of collisions is the "ionic wind" — and it produces measurable thrust.

Why This Matters

In 2018, MIT flew the first-ever solid-state airplane using ionic wind — no propeller, no turbine, no fuel burn. NASA's ongoing research suggests ion thrusters could enable whisper-quiet urban drones. You're building the fundamental physics behind the future of flight.

Safety First

High Voltage Warning

This project uses voltages between 20,000–30,000 volts. While the current from the power supply is low (typically under 1 mA), high voltage demands respect. A shock can cause involuntary muscle contraction, burns, or injury from falling. Never work alone. Never touch the circuit while powered. Always discharge capacitors before handling.

  • Always work with a buddy who knows where the power switch is.
  • Always use one hand when near the powered circuit (keeps current from crossing your heart).
  • Always unplug the power supply AND discharge the collector with a grounded resistor before touching anything.
  • Never operate near flammable materials, liquids, or in a wet environment.
  • Never leave the thruster powered unattended.
  • Work on a non-conductive surface (dry wood or rubber mat).
  • Wear rubber-soled shoes. Remove jewelry and watches.
  • Keep a fire extinguisher accessible (CO2 or dry chemical — NOT water).
  • If you're under 18, have a parent/guardian supervise every powered test.

How Ion Thrusters Work

The thruster relies on three fundamental physics principles:

1. Corona Discharge

When you apply a very high voltage to a thin wire (small radius of curvature), the electric field at the wire surface becomes intense enough to ionize nearby gas molecules. For air at atmospheric pressure, this happens at roughly 30 kV/cm at the wire surface. The thin wire geometry concentrates the field — a 0.1mm wire at 25kV easily exceeds this threshold.

2. Ion Drift & Momentum Transfer

The positive ions created at the corona wire accelerate toward the negative collector. As they travel through the 3–4cm air gap, each ion collides with thousands of neutral air molecules, transferring momentum to them. It's like a bowling ball (ion) plowing through a crowd of ping-pong balls (air molecules). The cumulative momentum transfer of billions of ions per second produces a measurable wind — and by Newton's third law, a thrust force on the electrodes.

3. The Thrust Equation

For an EHD thruster, thrust (F) is approximately proportional to the ion current (I) times the electrode gap distance (d), divided by ion mobility (μ):

F ≈ I × d / μ Where: F = thrust force (Newtons) I = ion current (Amps) — typically 0.1–0.5 mA d = electrode gap (meters) — typically 0.03–0.04 m μ = ion mobility in air ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁴ m²/(V·s) Example: I = 0.3mA, d = 0.03m F = (3×10⁻⁴ × 0.03) / (2×10⁻⁴) F = 0.045 N ≈ 4.6 grams-force
Key Insight

Thrust scales linearly with current and gap distance. To increase thrust: increase voltage (more current), lengthen your wire (more corona area), or optimize your gap distance. But there's a limit — if the gap is too small or voltage too high, you'll get an arc discharge (a spark) instead of smooth corona, which kills thrust and can damage the supply.

Step-by-Step Build

Build the Frame

Cut balsa sticks into four 25cm lengths for the main horizontal frame, and eight 4cm pieces for vertical supports. Glue the horizontal pieces into a rectangular frame (25cm × 10cm). Sand any rough edges.

The frame needs to be rigid but lightweight. Balsa is ideal — strong enough to hold the electrodes, light enough that thrust can move it.

Mount the Collector (Bottom Electrode)

Cut a strip of aluminum foil 3cm wide and long enough to wrap around the bottom perimeter of the frame (approximately 70cm). Fold the foil over the bottom balsa sticks so it forms a "skirt" hanging about 2.5cm below the frame on all sides. Secure with small dabs of super glue.

The collector should form a continuous conductive loop around the frame. Use a multimeter to verify continuity all the way around — any break will create a dead zone with no thrust.

Install Insulating Supports

Glue the 4cm vertical nylon standoffs or wooden dowels to the top of the frame at each corner, and one at the midpoint of each long side (6 total). These will hold the corona wire above the collector.

The gap between the corona wire and the top edge of the aluminum collector should be 3–4cm. This is your "acceleration gap." Too close → arc. Too far → weak field → low thrust.

String the Corona Wire (Emitter)

Run a single continuous strand of 36 AWG copper wire (or 0.1mm tungsten) around the tops of the standoffs, creating a wire loop above the collector. Keep the wire taut — any sag changes the local gap distance and creates uneven thrust.

Secure the wire to each standoff with a tiny notch cut into the top. Leave 15cm of extra wire at one end for the HV connection.

Wire the High-Voltage Power Supply

Connect the 12V DC adapter to the input terminals of the HV module. Connect the HV module's positive output to the corona wire using silicone-insulated wire and an alligator clip. Connect the negative output to the aluminum foil collector.

Solder all connections on the low-voltage side. On the HV side, use alligator clips wrapped in electrical tape. Route HV wires away from each other and away from the frame.

Before First Power-On

Triple-check: (1) No loose wires touching unintended surfaces. (2) HV connections are secure and insulated at junction points. (3) Your discharge resistor is wired across the HV output. (4) Your buddy is present and knows where the power switch is. (5) Nothing flammable is within 1 meter.

First Power Test

Place the thruster on a non-conductive surface. Stand back. Have your buddy at the switch. Power on the 12V supply. You should see and hear:

  • A faint hissing sound — this is the ionic wind.
  • A blue/purple glow around the corona wire in a dark room — this is the plasma discharge.
  • A slight breeze if you hold your hand 10cm below the collector (power off first to test the residual wind, or use a candle nearby to see deflection).
  • The smell of ozone — work in a ventilated area.

If you hear a loud snap or see a bright spark, that's an arc. Power off immediately. Your gap is too small or there's a sharp point on the collector creating a field concentration. Sand the collector edges smooth and increase the gap slightly.

Optimize for Maximum Thrust

Now iterate. This is where engineering happens:

  • Gap distance: Try 2.5cm, 3cm, 3.5cm, 4cm. Measure thrust at each. You'll find a sweet spot where corona is stable but field is strong.
  • Wire tension: Tighter wire = more uniform field = smoother thrust.
  • Collector geometry: Try different widths. A wider collector captures more ion flow.
  • Voltage: If your HV module is adjustable, sweep from 15kV up. Plot thrust vs. voltage. You should see a roughly linear increase until arcing begins.

Testing & Measurement

Measuring Thrust

Place the thruster on your precision scale (0.01g resolution). Record the weight with power off. Power on and record the new reading. The difference is your thrust force:

Thrust (grams-force) = Weight_off − Weight_on Example readings: Power OFF: 12.45g Power ON: 12.38g Thrust: 0.07g = 0.69 mN For your lab notebook, convert: 1 gram-force = 9.81 mN = 0.00981 N

Data Collection Protocol

  1. Let the scale stabilize for 30 seconds before each reading.
  2. Take 5 measurements at each setting and average them.
  3. Record: date, voltage setting, gap distance, ambient temperature, humidity.
  4. Photograph the setup at each configuration.
  5. Create a spreadsheet. Plot thrust vs. voltage, thrust vs. gap distance.
Pro Tip: Documenting for Competitions

If you're entering a science fair or competition, your data collection rigor matters as much as the project itself. Judges want to see: controlled variables, repeated trials, error bars on your graphs, and honest discussion of what didn't work. Take a photo of every test run. Film your blue glow in the dark — it's visually stunning and makes your presentation unforgettable.

Going Further

Multi-Stage Thruster

Stack two thrusters vertically — the output wind of the first stage feeds into the second. Measure whether thrust scales. This mimics multi-stage compressor design in real engines.

Asymmetric Electrode Design

Replace the flat collector with a curved airfoil profile. Test whether shaping the collector changes thrust direction or efficiency. This is active research at MIT.

Combine with P-002: Arduino Thrust Logger

Instead of manual scale readings, build the Arduino Thrust Logger and measure thrust continuously while sweeping voltage. Generate real-time thrust-vs-voltage curves automatically.

Vacuum Chamber Testing

If you can access or build a simple vacuum chamber (bell jar + hand pump), test how thrust changes with reduced air pressure. At lower pressures, mean free path increases — ions travel farther between collisions. You'll discover the fundamental tradeoff between atmospheric and space ion propulsion.

Write It Up

Document your results in a proper lab report or research paper format. If your data is solid, consider submitting to: your regional science fair, the Conrad Challenge, or Regeneron STS. Ionic wind propulsion is a genuinely active research area — your garage experiment is legitimate science.
